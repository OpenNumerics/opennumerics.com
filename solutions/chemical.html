Advanced PDE-Constrained Control of Chemical Reactors

The Problem

Modern chemical reactors operate at the intersection of heat transfer, fluid flow, and complex reaction kinetics. These physical and chemical processes are tightly coupled and highly nonlinear, meaning that small changes in operating conditions - inlet composition, temperature, or catalyst placement - can propagate through the reactor and lead to large variations in conversion, yield, energy consumption, and profitability.

One of our clients operates a gaseous carbon monoxide (CO) to oxygen (O₂) reactor, where catalyst pellets aid in CO conversion. Despite stable inlet conditions, they faced seemingly random temperature spikes that damaged the reactor and incurred a high cost of repair and downtime. The underlying challenge was a familiar one in reactor engineering: maximize the conversion yield while avoid thermal spikes and damage.

Exothermic reactions amplify this difficulty. Heat released by the reaction can accumulate locally, degrading catalyst beds, shortening reactor lifetime, or in extreme cases triggering runaway behavior. Achieving high performance without compromising safety requires deliberate, physics-aware control, and existing controllers were not up to the task.

This is precisely where our expertise adds value.

Our Appraoch

In this project, we optimized a gaseous CO-to-O₂ converter. The physical setup is shown in the figure above. On the left, a fixed mixture of CO and O₂ enters the reactor at a specified inlet temperature $T_{\text{in}}$. As the gas travels through the catalyzed region, CO is gradually converted into O₂. The objective is to minimize the CO concentration at the outlet. Increasing the inlet temperature accelerates the reaction and improves conversion. However, beyond a critical threshold, the system exhibits thermal runaway: the reaction releases heat, the temperature rises, which accelerates the reaction even further, leading to a sharp temperature spike. The second image shows this temperature spike. The transition is abrupt and displays hysteresis, meaning that once the reactor enters the high-temperature regime, it may not return to the safe branch even if the inlet temperature is reduced, an important operational and safety risk.

At Open Numerics, we built a custom controller that maximizes CO conversion while keeping the reactor safely away from runaway conditions. Our control input is the catalyst concentration $a(z)$, which determines how rapidly the reaction proceeds along the reactor. The reactor's temperature and species profiles are governed by first-principles PDEs describing transport, heat release, and reaction kinetics. These physics-based equations serve as constraints in the optimization, ensuring that every proposed solution respects the true thermochemical behavior of the system.

We solve the optimization problem $$\min_{a} J(a) = -\frac{\mathrm{CO}_{\text{out}}}{\mathrm{CO}_{\text{in}}} \;+\; \gamma \int \max(T(z) - T_{\text{in}}, 0)\, dz, $$ balancing high conversion with strict temperature control.

Our approach combines high-fidelity physics with cutting-edge optimization techniques. We use advanced finite-element PDE solvers and adjoint-based gradients to optimize controls efficiently and reliably, even in high-dimensional design spaces. The result is a solution that is optimal, physically interpretable, robust to uncertainty, and fully aligned with engineering safety limits by staying away from the upper branch.

For clients, this means safer operation, higher yield, larger profit margins, and the ability to explore design and control strategies digitally - before ever touching the physical reactor.
